  • Plastic's production not requiring time consuming manual work and its raw materials being cheap and widely available makes it too easy to mass produce.
  • Since 1907, we have produced over 8.3 billion metric tonnes of plastic. 360 million tonnes in 2018 alone.
  • More than 6.3 billion metric tonnes of plastic have become waste since its invention.
  • 9% of this waste has been recycled. 12% has been incinerated. 79% still remains across our planet.
  • 8 million tonnes of it ends up in the ocean. It is predicted to outweigh all the fish in the ocean by 2020

  • With how widespread plastic waste is, animals keep getting trapped in plastic and swallowing it. Many of them starve with stomachs full of indegestible garbage.
  • In 2015, 90% of seabirds had eaten plastic.
  • In 2018 a dead sperm whale washed up in Spain. It had eaten 32 KG of plastic bags, nets, and a drum.
  • Endangered wildlife like Hawaiian monk seals and Pacific loggerhead sea turtles are among nearly 700 species that eat and get caught in plastic litter.
  • Entanglement in plastic debris has also led to injury and mortality in the endangered Steller sea lion, with packing bands the most common entangling material.

  • Microplastics refer to pieces smaller than 5mm. 51 trillion such particles exist in the ocean.
  • Some are found in cosmetics, but most result from floating waste that is constantly exposed to UV radiation, which causes them to crumble into smaller pieces.
  • These particles travel up the food chain when they are swallowed by marine life.
  • Chemicals added to plastic are harmful to humans. BPA for example makes plastic more flexible, but also interferes with our hormonal system.
  • Studies conducted by researchers at the University of Newcastle in Australia, estimated that people consume about 5 grams of plastic a week - equivalent to that in a credit card
